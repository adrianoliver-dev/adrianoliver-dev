Marcus ran a clothing store across four locations — three storefronts and a central warehouse. On paper, the business worked. In practice, the back-office was chaos.

Every night, the manager at each location would WhatsApp the day's sales to a central number. Someone would manually update a shared spreadsheet. By the time the spreadsheet was updated, it was already wrong — because the next morning had already started.

Returns at one location never made it into the other locations' counts. Warehouse stock was always a guess. And at least once a week, a customer would order something online, pay, and then receive a call saying the item was actually out of stock.

This is not an unusual story for multi-location retail. It is, in fact, the default.

## The real cost of the spreadsheet system

The spreadsheet problem is not just inconvenient. It is expensive in ways that are hard to quantify until you add them up:

**Overselling** costs you the customer relationship, not just the refund. When someone pays for something and you call them to say you don't have it, they don't come back.

**Manual reconciliation** takes 2-3 hours at the end of every week. Multiply that by 52 weeks and you have an entire month of labor spent copying numbers between spreadsheets.

**Purchasing blind** means you only know you're out of stock when the shelf is empty. You reorder based on memory and instinct, not data.

**No audit trail** means when a discrepancy appears — and it always does — there is no way to know where it came from.

The business was growing. The system was not.

## What we built

The solution was a custom ERP and point-of-sale system built on a 19-table PostgreSQL schema, deployed on Next.js with Supabase as the database layer.

![Business Intelligence Center — real-time dashboard with revenue KPIs and low-stock alerts across all 4 locations](/images/projects/lukess-inventory/inventory_dashboard.png)

The most important design decision was this: **stock is never decremented directly on sale**. Instead, every sale triggers a database function that reserves the stock atomically — checking availability and reducing the count in a single transaction. If two sales happen simultaneously for the last unit in stock, one succeeds and one fails. Not both succeed, as they would in a spreadsheet system.

This is the fundamental technical guarantee that the business needed. Not faster spreadsheets. Not better messaging apps. A database-enforced contract that makes overselling architecturally impossible.

## How the system works day to day

Every morning, the manager at each location opens the POS interface on a tablet. They can see live stock counts across all four locations. When a sale is processed at the physical counter, inventory updates everywhere in real time — including the online storefront.

![Touch-optimized POS interface — SKU search, variant selection, and one-tap sale confirmation](/images/projects/lukess-inventory/inventory_POS_view.png)

When an online order comes in, it appears automatically in the orders section — separate from the POS — with all the customer's details, their chosen fulfillment method (shipping or in-store pickup), and payment status. The manager moves the order through its lifecycle: payment confirmed, preparing, ready for pickup or dispatched, completed. At each state change, the customer receives an automated notification by **email** — and optionally by WhatsApp if they opted in at checkout.

When an order is completed, the stock is permanently decremented and the sale recorded. If an order is cancelled, the reserved stock returns to available immediately.

At the end of the day, there is no reconciliation. The numbers are already correct.

The owner has a dashboard that shows revenue by location, low-stock alerts by SKU, and financial reports that can be exported to PDF or Excel in one click. What previously took a full weekend to compile manually is now available instantly.

## The architecture decisions that made this possible

**PostgreSQL triggers instead of application logic.** The stock reservation happens at the database level, not in the application. This means it cannot be bypassed — not by a bug in the application, not by two simultaneous API calls, not by anything.

**Row-Level Security for multi-role access.** Staff at one location can process sales but cannot see the financial reports. Managers can adjust stock but cannot delete products. The owner has full access. These permissions are enforced at the database level, not just in the UI.

**Supabase Realtime for live sync.** When a sale happens at Location A, the inventory count at Location B updates without anyone refreshing the page. This is WebSocket-based, not polling — the latency is under 200 milliseconds.

**No third-party inventory platform.** Every feature in the system was built specifically for this business's workflow. There is no monthly SaaS subscription, no per-location pricing, and no features that exist only to justify a pricing tier.

## What changed

Before the system, the business was running on three disconnected spreadsheets, messages, and best guesses. Returns were unrecorded. Purchasing was reactive. Financial visibility was zero.

After: every stock movement, every sale, every return, and every staff action is logged with a timestamp and user ID. The record is immutable. The owner knows exactly what is in stock at every location, in real time, from any device.

The automated email notification on every order state change — confirmed, preparing, dispatched, delivered — eliminated an entire category of customer service follow-ups. Customers know where their order stands without having to ask.

## Is this the right solution for your business?

A custom inventory system makes sense when:

- You have more than two locations managing shared stock
- Overselling is a recurring problem with real customer impact
- You are spending significant staff time on manual reconciliation
- You need financial reporting that reflects real-time data, not end-of-week summaries

It does not make sense when you have a single location, low order volume, or a business model where stock discrepancies are rare and low-stakes.

If you recognize your business in the first list, the spreadsheet system is not a temporary problem. It is a permanent drag on your operations that grows more expensive as you scale.
