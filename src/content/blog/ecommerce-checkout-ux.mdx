The average e-commerce cart abandonment rate is around 70%. For apparel, it is often higher because of the specific friction that comes with size uncertainty, return anxiety, and payment method mismatches.

Most of the abandonment happens in the last three steps: cart review, checkout form, and payment. These are the moments where the customer has already decided to buy — and the store loses them anyway.

Here are the five mistakes that appear most consistently in apparel checkouts, and what to do instead.

## 1. Asking for an account before the customer has paid

This is the most common and most damaging checkout mistake. The customer adds items to the cart, clicks checkout, and the first screen they see is a login wall or a registration form.

The research on this is not ambiguous. The "guest checkout" option consistently outperforms forced registration by 30-50% in conversion rate. For a first-time customer, account creation is friction. They do not yet trust you enough to create a relationship. They just want to buy the thing.

The correct flow: let them checkout with just an email and a phone number. Create the account automatically if you want one, or offer it as an optional post-purchase step. Never gate the purchase behind account creation.

The Lukess Home checkout has no mandatory account creation. A customer can complete a purchase with a name and a phone number. Repeat customers can log in to access order history, but it is never required.

## 2. Not showing size guides at the point of decision

Customers abandon at the product page or the cart when they are not confident about their size. This is especially true for brands without a strong fit reputation, and for customers buying online for the first time.

The mistake is placing the size guide in the FAQ, the footer, or a separate page. By the time the customer navigates there, the purchase flow is broken.

The correct placement: a size guide link or inline pop-up directly adjacent to the size selector. The customer should not have to leave the product page to get the information they need to commit to a size.

This is also a returns issue. Wrong-size returns are the most common return reason in apparel. Every size guide click that happens at the point of decision is a potential return prevented.

## 3. Too many form fields

Standard checkout forms ask for: first name, last name, email, phone, address line 1, address line 2, city, state, zip, country. That is ten fields before payment.

Every additional field is a decision point where the customer can stop. The research consistently shows that reducing form fields from ten to five increases completion rates significantly.

For apparel specifically, many brands do not need half these fields. If you only ship domestically, you do not need the country field. If you have a phone number for WhatsApp or SMS notifications, you may not need email. If you use a payment provider that handles addresses, you may be able to skip address fields entirely in the checkout form.

Audit your checkout form. Remove every field that is not strictly necessary to fulfill the order. The form should ask for exactly what you need — nothing more.

## 4. Unclear shipping costs and timelines

Customers abandon when they reach the payment step and see a shipping cost they did not expect. This is called "checkout surprise" and it is the second-largest source of cart abandonment after the account wall.

The fix is simple: show shipping costs before the customer starts the checkout form. Display them on the product page, in the cart, and at every step of checkout. Never introduce a new cost after the customer has committed mentally to a price.

Similarly, unclear or missing delivery timelines create anxiety. "Ships in 3-7 business days" is better than nothing, but "Ships today before 3PM, arrives Thursday" is far better. The more specific the timeline, the more confident the customer feels about completing the purchase.

## 5. Missing or mismatched payment methods

Payment method mismatch is the most market-specific checkout problem. What works in the US does not work everywhere.

In the US and EU, card and PayPal coverage is near-universal. In Latin America, bank transfers, QR codes, and cash-on-delivery are common and often preferred. A checkout that only offers credit card payment will lose customers who do not have or do not trust credit cards online.

The Lukess Home checkout offers three payment paths: credit/debit card via Stripe, QR bank transfer for customers who prefer direct payment, and cash pickup for in-store collection. The right set of payment methods depends entirely on your customer base.

If you are building for a specific market, research the payment preferences of that market before finalizing your checkout flow. Do not assume that your own payment habits represent your customers'.

---

## A note on measurement

All of these optimizations are only worth making if you measure them. Add a step-by-step funnel to your analytics — from product page to add-to-cart to checkout start to payment to completion. Find the step with the highest drop-off rate and fix that one first.

Improving checkout UX is not a one-time project. It is an ongoing practice of measuring where customers stop and removing the friction that stopped them.
